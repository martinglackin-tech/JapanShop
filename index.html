An interactive tool to support staff in assessing mental capacity.

The 5 Principles of the Mental Capacity Act

Before you begin, remember the five statutory principles that are the foundation of the Mental Capacity Act 2005. They are designed to protect and empower individuals who may lack capacity.

1. Start by assuming they CAN make the decision: Always begin by believing that an adult can make their own choice, unless there's clear evidence to show they can't.
2. Help them to make their own decision: Before you decide someone can't make a decision, you must give them all the support they need. This could mean using simple language, pictures, or giving them more time.
3. It's okay to make 'unwise' choices: Everyone has the right to make a decision that others might think is strange or risky. This doesn't automatically mean they can't make a decision.
4. Act in their best interests: If you have to make a decision for someone who can't, it must be what's best for them, not what's easiest for you.
5. Choose the option with the most freedom: If you have to act for someone, you must choose the option that interferes the least with their freedom and rights.

Step 4: Coercive Control Check

What is Coercive Control?

Coercive control is a pattern of behaviour by an abuser to harm, punish, or frighten their victim. It can include intimidation, isolation, threats, and controlling every-day life. This can significantly impact a person's ability to make a free and independent decision, even if they appear to have capacity.

Do you have any concerns about the impact of coercive control on this person's decision-making?

Important Consideration: Coercive Control

Because you have concerns about coercive control, it is important to consider the following implications for your practice.

Implications for practice:

  • Be aware that the person may be adapting their decisions to minimise risk of harm.
  • Remember that the person knows their situation best. Do not impose or force a decision; focus on building trust.
  • Your professional skills are crucial in creating a safe space for the person to make their own decisions.
  • Always be clear about any indicators of coercive control when raising a concern with the local authority.
